HERO_HEADING
Names matter. Let’s make them easy.

HERO_BODY
Names are how we find each other at TRU—on the first day of class, in a lab group, at the service counter, in an email thread, or on a Teams call where someone’s mic cuts out at the worst moment. Names are also often the first thing we try to say out loud about someone. That moment can land as welcome, or as distance.

Learning and using someone’s name the way they prefer is a practical, everyday way to show respect. It doesn’t need to be stiff or formal. It can be a small, friendly practice—one that gets easier with permission, patience, and a bit of shared humour when it fits.

At TRU, respectful name use helps campus feel more human: fewer awkward guesses, fewer repeat corrections, and more ease in the small moments that make up a day.

### Quick guide

• Ask with permission: “Can I check how you say your name?”
• Give choice: “Want to tell me, or should I try first?”
• Practice together: “Mind if I try that once more?”
• Use it naturally: Say it again in your next sentence.
• Correct kindly: “It’s ___.” (No story required.)

WHY_HEADING
Why names matter

WHY_BODY
A name can carry identity, family, language, and culture. It can also carry memory—of being welcomed, overlooked, shortened, joked about, or repeatedly “almost” said right.

Names also connect to power in everyday ways. Who gets asked how to say their name—and who gets guessed at? Who is treated as “too hard,” and who is given extra care?

Respecting names is not about getting everything perfect. It’s about choosing a better default:

• Curiosity over guessing
• Practice over performance
• Consent over convenience
• Repair over embarrassment

LEARNING_HEADING
Learning names

LEARNING_BODY
Learning names is a social skill, not a test. Most people aren’t looking for perfect pronunciation on the first try—they’re looking for effort, respect, and a willingness to learn.

### A practical approach

1. Ask with permission
2. Listen once without jumping in
3. Invite shared practice
4. Use the name naturally

SHARING_HEADING
Sharing your name

SHARING_BODY
You have the right to be addressed the way you prefer. You also get to choose how much you want to teach, repeat, or explain.

### Simple ways to help others learn

1. Lead with what you want to be called
2. Add a cue that fits your style
3. Make it easier in writing
4. Correct in a way that feels safe

BARRIERS_HEADING
Barriers and bridges

BARRIERS_BODY
Barrier: I’m nervous to ask because it might feel awkward.
Bridge: Ask with permission and warmth.

Barrier: I’m worried I’ll say it wrong.
Bridge: Make it collaborative.

Barrier: I guessed and now need to fix it.
Bridge: Keep it calm and human.
