Names matter. Let’s make them easy.

<!--/BLOCK:HERO\_HEADING-->



<!--BLOCK:HERO\_BODY-->

Names are how we find each other at TRU—on the first day of class, in a lab group, at the service counter, in an email thread, or on a Teams call where someone’s mic cuts out at the worst moment. Names are also often the first thing we try to say out loud about someone. That moment can land as welcome, or as distance.



Learning and using someone’s name the way they prefer is a practical, everyday way to show respect. It doesn’t need to be stiff or formal. It can be a small, friendly practice—one that gets easier with permission, patience, and a bit of shared humour when it fits.



At TRU, respectful name use helps campus feel more human: fewer awkward guesses, fewer repeat corrections, and more ease in the small moments that make up a day.



\### Quick guide



\* \*\*Ask with permission:\*\* “Can I check how you say your name?”

\* \*\*Give choice:\*\* “Want to tell me, or should I try first?”

\* \*\*Practice together:\*\* “Mind if I try that once more?”

\* \*\*Use it naturally:\*\* Say it again in your next sentence.

\* \*\*Correct kindly:\*\* “It’s \_\_\_.” (No story required.)



\### What “respectful” looks like



\* You ask early, without making it a big production.

\* You don’t guess based on how it looks or where you think it comes from.

\* You keep your tone warm and normal—like learning any new word.

\* You make room for correction and adjustment over time.

<!--BLOCK:WHY\_HEADING-->

Why names matter

<!--/BLOCK:WHY\_HEADING-->



<!--BLOCK:WHY\_BODY-->

A name can carry identity, family, language, and culture. It can also carry memory—of being welcomed, overlooked, shortened, joked about, or repeatedly “almost” said right. For some people, hearing their name said well is quietly relieving. For others, being renamed without consent can feel like being filed down to fit the room.



Names also connect to power in everyday ways. Who gets asked how to say their name—and who gets guessed at? Who is treated as “too hard,” and who is given extra care? Who has to do the work of correction, again and again, while also trying to learn, teach, lead, or serve? These patterns are often unintentional. They still shape belonging.



At TRU, names show up everywhere: class lists, advising appointments, group work, email signatures, learning platforms, Teams calls, name tags, and records in campus systems. Small moments—roll call, introductions, calling on someone in class, writing an email, adding someone to a document—can either create ease or create distance.



Respecting names is not about getting everything perfect. It’s about choosing a better default:



\* \*\*Curiosity over guessing\*\*

\* \*\*Practice over performance\*\*

\* \*\*Consent over convenience\*\*

\* \*\*Repair over embarrassment\*\*



When we do that consistently, campus interactions feel smoother. People spend less time bracing, correcting, or shrinking. And everyone gets more room to focus on learning, work, and community.

<!--BLOCK:LEARNING\_HEADING-->

Learning names

<!--/BLOCK:LEARNING\_HEADING-->



<!--BLOCK:LEARNING\_BODY-->

Learning names is a social skill, not a test. Most people aren’t looking for perfect pronunciation on the first try—they’re looking for effort, respect, and a willingness to learn. The goal is comfort: the kind where people don’t brace for their name, and where you don’t feel you have to “perform” correctness.



This approach works in classrooms, meetings, advising sessions, labs, residence life, athletics, and front-facing services. It also works when you’re tired, rushed, or managing a room full of people.



\### A practical approach



1\. \*\*Ask with permission.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Try: “Can I check how you say your name?”  

&nbsp;  Try: “What name would you like me to use?”  

&nbsp;  If you’re leading a group, you can set a supportive norm: “Please correct me if I miss your name. I appreciate it.”



2\. \*\*Listen once without jumping in.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Give the person a clean first run. Don’t interrupt or “fill it in.”  

&nbsp;  Then repeat it back at a normal pace (not overly slow, not overly careful).



3\. \*\*Invite a shared practice moment.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Try: “Did I get that close?”  

&nbsp;  Try: “Mind if I try it one more time?”  

&nbsp;  If it’s tricky, ask for a coaching format: “Could you break it into parts?” or “Which part should I stress (emphasize)?”



4\. \*\*Use it naturally—and keep learning.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Use the name in the next sentence: “Thanks, \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  The second use is often where it sticks. If you slip later, it’s okay to check again: “Can I confirm your name again?”



\### Tips that help in real life



\* \*\*Syllables:\*\* “Can you break it into parts?” Then try each part once, then the full name.

\* \*\*Emphasis (stress):\*\* “Which part should I stress?” or “Where does the emphasis go?”

\* \*\*Pace:\*\* “Can you say it once more, a bit slower?” (Especially helpful in noisy settings.)

\* \*\*Spelling (for writing):\*\* “How do you spell it?” Use this for email, documents, or lists.

\* \*\*A quiet reminder:\*\* If it fits the context, jot a private note right after (for example: “emphasis on second syllable”).

\* \*\*Audio context:\*\* In online meetings, ask for a re-say once the connection is stable, or ask for syllables in chat.



\### If you’re not sure what you heard



A short pause is respectful. Rushing is what causes the worst guesses.



\* Try: “I want to say it the way you prefer—can you help me?”

\* Try: “Could you say it once more?”

\* Try: “I heard \_\_\_—is that right?”  

&nbsp; If you’re still unsure, name it simply: “I’m still learning. Thank you.”



\### If you’re teaching or facilitating



You can make name learning easier for everyone without turning it into a performance.



\* \*\*Offer options:\*\* “Share your name and pronunciation if you’d like.”

\* \*\*Give a low-pressure correction norm:\*\* “Quick corrections are welcome.”

\* \*\*Use names in context:\*\* Call on people using the name they shared, then use it again when you respond.

\* \*\*Avoid spotlighting:\*\* If you miss a name, correct briefly and continue without making it the centre of attention.

<!--BLOCK:SHARING\_HEADING-->

Sharing your name

<!--/BLOCK:SHARING\_HEADING-->



<!--BLOCK:SHARING\_BODY-->

You have the right to be addressed the way you prefer. You also get to choose how much you want to teach, repeat, or explain—especially in busy or public settings. Sharing your name can be quick, detailed, playful, private, or minimal—whatever fits the moment and your comfort.



You don’t owe anyone a story about your name’s origin, meaning, or background. A preference is enough.



\### Simple ways to help others learn (if you want to)



1\. \*\*Lead with what you want to be called.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Try: “Hi, I’m \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  Try: “I go by \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  Try: “Please call me \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  If your preferred name differs from what appears on a list, you can say: “I’m listed as \_\_\_, but I go by \_\_\_.”



2\. \*\*Add a cue that fits your style.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Options include:



&nbsp;  \* \*\*Syllables:\*\* “It’s \_\_\_ / \_\_\_.”

&nbsp;  \* \*\*Emphasis (stress):\*\* “The stress is on \_\_\_.”

&nbsp;  \* \*\*Pace:\*\* “It helps if you say it a bit slower at first.”

&nbsp;  \* \*\*A ‘sounds like’ hint:\*\* Only if you enjoy using one. You can also skip this entirely.



3\. \*\*Make it easier in writing (optional).\*\*  

&nbsp;  If it helps, you can add a cue in places you control, like an email signature or display name. Some people include pronunciation; some don’t. Either is fine. What matters is what you prefer to be called in conversation.



4\. \*\*Correct in a way that feels safe for you.\*\*  

&nbsp;  Quick and kind works well:  

&nbsp;  Try: “It’s \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  Try: “Almost—it’s \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  Try: “Thanks for checking. It’s \_\_\_.”  

&nbsp;  If you’re correcting in writing: “Quick note—my name is spelled \_\_\_.”



\### Different situations, different corrections



\* \*\*In a classroom or group:\*\* “It’s \_\_\_.” (Then continue.)

\* \*\*One-on-one:\*\* “Can I correct that? It’s \_\_\_.”

\* \*\*In email:\*\* “Quick correction on my name: \_\_\_.”

\* \*\*If someone is trying:\*\* “You’re close—thanks. It’s \_\_\_.”

\* \*\*If someone keeps using a nickname you didn’t choose:\*\* “Please call me \_\_\_.”



\### If you’re tired of correcting



That’s real. You can correct less, correct later, or choose the moments that feel safest. If you do correct, a simple line is enough—and you don’t have to manage anyone’s feelings about it.



If you want support setting the norm (for example in a class, team, or service environment), it’s reasonable to ask a facilitator, instructor, or supervisor to help reinforce respectful name use in the group.

<!--BLOCK:BARRIERS\_HEADING-->

Barriers and bridges

<!--/BLOCK:BARRIERS\_HEADING-->



<!--BLOCK:BARRIERS\_BODY-->

\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m nervous to ask because it might feel awkward.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Ask with permission and warmth: “Can I check how you say your name?” Asking early is usually less awkward than guessing later.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m worried I’ll say it wrong and offend someone.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Make it collaborative: “Help me get it right?” Try once, then ask, “Mind if I try it again?” That’s often safer than guessing and hoping.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I guessed, and now I need to fix it.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Keep it calm and human: “Thanks for your patience—can you say it once more?” Repeat it back once, then carry on with a steady tone.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m afraid of making a big deal out of someone’s name.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Treat it like any learning moment: brief, respectful, normal. A warm tone plus a simple question is often enough.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I keep forgetting names, especially in big groups.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Use the name right away: “Thanks, \_\_\_.” If appropriate, make a quick private note after (for example: emphasis or syllables) so you improve next time.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m teaching and I don’t want corrections to derail the class.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Set a norm early: “Quick corrections are welcome.” If you miss a name, correct once, thank them, and continue without spotlighting.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m at a busy service counter and there’s no time.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Use a fast check: “How do you say your name?” If time is very tight, ask, “What name should I use today?” and follow their lead.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m dyslexic or reading unfamiliar names is hard.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Lead with listening: “Can you say your name for me?” Ask for syllables or emphasis instead of relying on spelling, unless you need it in writing.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I have a speech difference, and some sounds are hard for me.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Be simple and direct: “I may need a moment to practice.” Ask for parts or a slower pace, and do one or two tries—no performance, no pressure.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m hard of hearing, or the space is loud.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Reduce noise if you can, or ask for the name in writing. Confirm gently: “Did you say \_\_\_?” and let them correct you.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* Online audio is glitchy and I can’t catch it clearly.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Use chat as backup: “Could you type the syllables?” You can also ask to confirm once the connection is stable: “Can I check your name again now that audio is clear?”



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I don’t want to put someone on the spot in front of a group.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Offer a private option: “Could I check your name after class?” If you need it now, keep it brief and normal, not performative.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m the person with the name, and I’m tired of correcting people.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Choose the lowest-effort correction that works: “It’s \_\_\_.” If you want, add: “Thanks for adjusting.” You don’t need to explain.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* Someone keeps using a nickname I didn’t choose.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* State your preference plainly: “Please call me \_\_\_.” Repeat once if needed. You don’t owe justification.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* My name was misspelled in an email or document.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Correct it in the same channel: “Quick note—my name is spelled \_\_\_.” If it’s shared widely, ask for an updated version.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* When I correct someone, they apologize a lot, and it gets uncomfortable.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Give them a gracious exit: “All good—thanks for trying. It’s \_\_\_.” Then steer back to the topic so the moment doesn’t linger.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’ve missed it more than once and I feel embarrassed to try again.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Normalize learning: “I’m still practicing—thanks for helping.” Ask for one more slow repeat, then use the name confidently next time.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m worried I’m bothering someone by asking.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Offer choice: “Would you like to tell me how to say your name, or should I try first?” Let them decide how much coaching they want.



\*\*Barrier:\*\* I’m not sure whether to correct pronunciation or spelling.  

\*\*Bridge:\*\* Match the channel. In conversation, correct pronunciation: “It’s \_\_\_.” In writing, correct spelling: “My name is spelled \_\_\_.” You can do both, but you don’t have to do it all at once.
