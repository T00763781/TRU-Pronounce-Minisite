HERO\_HEADING
Names matter. Let’s make them easy.

HERO\_BODY
At TRU, most days begin with names—an instructor calling roll, students greeting peers, friends sharing a moment between classes. When your name is said the way you expect to hear it, the moment opens a little: less hesitation, fewer awkward corrections, more ease in day-to-day life. Those small interactions add up. When people hear their name said the way they prefer, it signals attention, care, and a place of belonging.

We often meet each other through names first: on the first day of class, in a group project, in an email thread, or on a Teams call where someone’s mic cuts out at the worst moment. A name is often the first thing we try to say out loud about someone. That moment can land as welcome or as distance. A quick check or a thoughtful attempt can turn uncertainty into respect.

Learning and using someone’s name the way they prefer is a simple, practical way to show respect. It does not need to be stiff or formal. It can be a small, friendly habit, getting easier with patience, practice, and sometimes a bit of shared humour. Like many social skills, it improves with repetition and becomes more natural over time.

### Quick guide

A good approach is simple: ask early, keep it normal, and make room for correction. Most people are not looking for perfect pronunciation on the first try. They are looking for effort, respect, and a willingness to adjust.

* Ask with permission (early, without turning it into a big production)
* Offer choice (tell you, or you try first)
* Practice once, then repeat it naturally
* Use the name again in your next sentence
* Correct and continue (thank them, carry on)

LEARNING\_HEADING
Learning names

LEARNING\_BODY
Learning names is a social skill, not a test. People rarely remember the exact sound of your first attempt, but they do remember the feeling: whether you treated their name as worth learning.

### A facilitation sequence that works (in classrooms, meetings, services)

1. **Set the norm once.** Invite quick corrections and make it safe to adjust.
2. **Ask early.** Early checks prevent a wrong guess from becoming a habit.
3. **Listen once, reflect once.** Repeat the name back at a normal pace.
4. **Practice one more time.** A second attempt is often where it sticks.
5. **Use it immediately.** Use the name in the next sentence so it lands in memory.
6. **Repair quickly.** Correct, thank, and return to the topic.

### Scripts (choose the lightest one that fits)

* “Could you help me say your name the way you prefer?”
* “I want to get this right. Can I try it once more?”
* “Which part should I put the emphasis on?”
* “Would you prefer I use your full name, or a shorter version?”

### Tips that help in real life

* **Syllables:** Ask for parts, then say the full name once.
* **Emphasis:** Ask where the stress goes.
* **Pace:** Ask for one slower repeat in noisy settings.
* **Memory:** If appropriate, make a private note right after (syllables or emphasis).

SHARING\_HEADING
Sharing your name

SHARING\_BODY
You have the right to be addressed the way you prefer. You also get to choose how much you want to teach, repeat, or explain. A preference is enough.

### A simple way to teach your name (if you want to)

1. **Say it once normally.** Then once more a little slower.
2. **Break it into parts.** Two or three syllables is usually enough.
3. **Add a cue (optional).** A “like…” hint can help, but you can skip it.
4. **Invite correction.** You can ask people to try again without making it a big moment.

### Scripts that protect your energy

* “I go by \_\_\_.”
* “It’s \_\_\_ (stress on \_\_\_).”
* “Thanks for checking. It’s \_\_\_.”
* “Please call me \_\_\_.” (That’s a complete sentence.)

### If you are facilitating introductions

* Offer options: spoken, chat, or private correction.
* Avoid asking for origins or meanings.
* Normalize updates: names and preferences can change.

BARRIERS\_HEADING
Barriers and bridges

BARRIERS\_BODY
Below are common barriers people feel, and small bridges that help. Use the script if it fits your voice.

### Barrier: “I’m nervous to ask because it might feel awkward.”

* **Bridge:** Asking early is usually less awkward than guessing later.
* **Script:** “Can I quickly check how you say your name?”

### Barrier: “I’m worried I’ll say it wrong and offend someone.”

* **Bridge:** Make it collaborative and low-stakes.
* **Script:** “Help me get this right. Can I try it once more?”

### Barrier: “I guessed, and now I need to fix it.”

* **Bridge:** Repair calmly and move on.
* **Script:** “Thanks for your patience. Could you say it again?”

### Barrier: “I don’t want to make a big deal out of someone’s name.”

* **Bridge:** Treat it like learning any new word: brief, normal, respectful.
* **Script:** “I want to say it the way you prefer. How do you pronounce it?”

### Barrier: “I keep forgetting names, especially in big groups.”

* **Bridge:** Use the name immediately and, if appropriate, make a private note.
* **Script:** “Thanks, \_\_\_.” (Use it again right away.)

### Barrier: “I’m teaching and I don’t want corrections to derail the class.”

* **Bridge:** Set a norm early so corrections stay short.
* **Script:** “Quick corrections are welcome. Thank you.”

### Barrier: “I’m at a busy service counter and there’s no time.”

* **Bridge:** Use a fast preference check.
* **Script:** “What name should I use today?”

### Barrier: “Reading unfamiliar names is hard for me.”

* **Bridge:** Lead with listening, not guessing from spelling.
* **Script:** “Could you say your name for me? I want to get it right.”

### Barrier: “I have a speech difference; some sounds are hard.”

* **Bridge:** Name it simply and ask for coaching without turning it into a performance.
* **Script:** “I may need a moment to practice. Could you break it into parts?”

### Barrier: “The space is loud, or I’m hard of hearing.”

* **Bridge:** Reduce noise if possible, or confirm in writing.
* **Script:** “Could you type the syllables, or write it down for me?”

### Barrier: “Online audio is glitchy and I can’t catch it clearly.”

* **Bridge:** Use chat as backup and confirm once audio is stable.
* **Script:** “Could you type how you say it? I’ll repeat it back.”

### Barrier: “Someone keeps using a nickname I didn’t choose.”

* **Bridge:** State your preference plainly; no justification required.
* **Script:** “Please call me \_\_\_.”
