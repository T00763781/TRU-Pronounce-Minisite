HERO_HEADING
Names matter. They’re part of how we make TRU a place of belonging.

HERO_BODY
At Thompson Rivers University, most days begin with names. An instructor calling roll. A staff member greeting someone at a counter. A teammate checking in on a call. When your name is said the way you prefer, the moment becomes a little easier. You spend less time bracing or correcting, and more time learning, working, and connecting.

Learning names can feel uncomfortable at first. Many of us worry about getting it wrong, slowing things down, or drawing attention to the moment. But learning names is also a normal social skill, one we can all get better at through practice. Perfection isn’t the goal. Effort, respect, and willingness to adjust are what matter.

This tab is here to help make that work easier. You’ll find short stories, practical guidance for learning and sharing names, and a simple tool you can use as a starting point.

### Quick guide
A good approach is simple: ask early, keep it warm and ordinary, and make room for correction. Most people aren’t grading your first attempt. They’re noticing whether you care enough to try, and whether you’re willing to adjust.

• Ask with permission, early  
• Offer choice: tell you, or you try first  
• Listen once, then repeat once at a normal pace  
• Practice briefly, then use the name naturally  
• If corrected, thank them and carry on

LEARNING_HEADING
Learning names

LEARNING_BODY
Learning names is a social skill, not a test. The goal is not flawless pronunciation on the first try. The goal is to create ease: a campus where people feel recognized and don’t have to “prepare” for their name.

### A practical sequence (the “4‑step loop”)
1. **Ask with permission.** Ask early, before a guess becomes a habit.  
2. **Listen once, then reflect.** Let the person say it cleanly, then repeat it back at a normal pace.  
3. **Invite one more try.** A second attempt often helps it stick.  
4. **Use it right away.** Use the name in your next sentence, then again later.

### In-the-moment scripts (pick the lightest one that fits)
• “Could you help me pronounce your name the way you prefer?”  
• “I want to get this right. Can I try it once more?”  
• “Where does the emphasis go?”  
• “Would you like to tell me, or should I try first?”

### Facilitation guidance (instructors, hosts, front-facing staff)
**Before introductions:** set a brief norm once.  
• “Quick corrections are welcome. Thank you for helping me learn.”

**During introductions:** give options and reduce spotlighting.  
• Allow people to share pronunciation if they want, in voice or chat.  
• Offer a private path for corrections (“message me after”).

**When you miss a name:** repair quickly and return to the topic.  
• Correct → thank → continue. Avoid long apologies that shift attention onto you.

SHARING_HEADING
Sharing your name

SHARING_BODY
You deserve to be addressed the way you prefer. You also get to choose how much you want to teach, repeat, or explain. A preference is enough.

### If you want to help someone learn your name
1. Say it once normally, then once a bit slower.  
2. Break it into parts (two or three pieces is usually enough).  
3. Add emphasis (“stress on the ___”) if helpful.  
4. Invite a second try.

### Scripts that protect your energy
• “I go by ___.”  
• “It’s pronounced ___.”  
• “Almost. It’s ___.”  
• “Please call me ___.” (That’s a complete sentence.)

### Supporting others to share (facilitation)
• Offer multiple ways to share (spoken, chat, private note).  
• Don’t ask for origins, meanings, or “where it’s from.”  
• Normalize updates: names and preferences can change over time.

BARRIERS_HEADING
Barriers and bridges

BARRIERS_BODY
Below are common barriers people feel, and small bridges that help. Use the script if it fits your voice.

1. **Barrier:** “I’m nervous to ask. It might feel awkward.”  
   **Bridge:** Asking early is usually less awkward than guessing later.  
   **Script:** “Can I quickly check how you say your name?”

2. **Barrier:** “I’m worried I’ll say it wrong and offend someone.”  
   **Bridge:** Make it collaborative and low-stakes.  
   **Script:** “Help me get this right. Can I try it once more?”

3. **Barrier:** “I guessed, and now I need to fix it.”  
   **Bridge:** Repair calmly and move on.  
   **Script:** “Thanks for your patience. Could you say it again?”

4. **Barrier:** “I don’t want to make a big deal out of someone’s name.”  
   **Bridge:** Treat it like learning any new word: brief, normal, respectful.  
   **Script:** “I want to say it the way you prefer. How do you pronounce it?”

5. **Barrier:** “I forget names, especially in big groups.”  
   **Bridge:** Use the name right away; a second use helps it stick.  
   **Script:** “Thanks, ___.” (Use it again naturally in your next sentence.)

6. **Barrier:** “Corrections derail the flow when I’m teaching or facilitating.”  
   **Bridge:** Set a norm once so corrections stay short.  
   **Script:** “Quick corrections are welcome. Thank you.”

7. **Barrier:** “It’s a busy service environment. There’s no time.”  
   **Bridge:** Use a fast preference check.  
   **Script:** “What name should I use today?”

8. **Barrier:** “Reading unfamiliar names is hard for me.”  
   **Bridge:** Lead with listening, not guessing from spelling.  
   **Script:** “Could you say your name for me? I want to get it right.”

9. **Barrier:** “I have a speech difference; some sounds are hard.”  
   **Bridge:** Name it simply and ask for coaching without turning it into a performance.  
   **Script:** “I may need a moment to practice. Could you break it into parts?”

10. **Barrier:** “The space is loud / I’m hard of hearing.”  
    **Bridge:** Reduce noise or confirm in writing.  
    **Script:** “Could you type the syllables or write it down so I can repeat it back?”

11. **Barrier:** “Online audio is glitchy and I can’t catch it.”  
    **Bridge:** Use chat as backup and confirm once audio is clear.  
    **Script:** “Could you type how you say it? I’ll repeat it back.”

12. **Barrier:** “Someone keeps using a nickname I didn’t choose.”  
    **Bridge:** State your preference plainly; no justification required.  
    **Script:** “Please call me ___.”
