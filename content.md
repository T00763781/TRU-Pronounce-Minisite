HERO\_HEADING

Names matter. Learning them is something we do together.



HERO\_BODY

At Thompson Rivers University, belonging begins with small everyday moments.  

One of the first is hearing your name said the way you expect to hear it.



In classrooms, offices, hallways, and online meetings, names are often the first thing we say to one another. When someone takes the time to learn your name the way you prefer, it signals attention, respect, and care. Those moments help create the sense of community TRU works to build every day.



Learning names is not about perfect pronunciation the first time. It is a social skill we practice together. With curiosity, patience, and a willingness to adjust, it becomes easier and more natural over time.



This page gathers a few tools, stories, and quick practices to make learning and sharing names simpler for everyone in our community.

LEARNING\_HEADING

Learning names



LEARNING\_BODY

Learning names can feel uncomfortable at first. Many of us worry about getting it wrong or slowing down a conversation.



At TRU, we encourage a different approach: treat learning names as a normal part of meeting someone new. When asking about pronunciation becomes routine, the moment quickly becomes easy and respectful.



\### A simple approach



1\. \*\*Ask early.\*\* A quick check prevents a guess from becoming a habit.

2\. \*\*Listen once, repeat once.\*\* Reflect the name back at a normal pace.

3\. \*\*Practice briefly.\*\* One more attempt often helps it stick.

4\. \*\*Use the name naturally.\*\* Say it again in your next sentence.

5\. \*\*Adjust if needed.\*\* A quick correction keeps the conversation moving.



\### Helpful ways to ask



\* “Could you help me pronounce your name the way you prefer?”

\* “I want to make sure I’m saying it right.”

\* “Where should the emphasis go?”



These small moments of curiosity and respect help make our campus a place where people feel recognized and included.

SHARING\_HEADING

Sharing your name



SHARING\_BODY

Everyone deserves to be addressed the way they prefer. Sharing your name can be simple and quick, and you get to choose how much you want to explain.



\### If you want to help someone learn your name



1\. Say it once at a normal pace.

2\. Repeat it once more a little slower.

3\. Break it into parts if that helps.

4\. Invite them to try again.



Many people also share pronunciation in email signatures, introductions, or short recordings. These small tools can make introductions easier and reduce uncertainty.



\### Simple ways to share your preference



\* “I go by \_\_\_.”

\* “It’s pronounced \_\_\_.”

\* “Please call me \_\_\_.”



You do not need to explain further unless you want to.

BARRIERS\_HEADING

Barriers and bridges



BARRIERS\_BODY

Many people want to learn names respectfully but are unsure how to begin. Below are common concerns and small ways to move forward.



\### “I’m worried about getting it wrong.”



Mistakes happen when learning any new word. What matters most is the willingness to try and adjust.



\*\*Try:\*\*  

“Thanks for your patience — could you say it once more?”



---



\### “I don’t want to make a big deal out of someone’s name.”



Learning a name can be quick and ordinary. A short check often feels more natural than guessing.



\*\*Try:\*\*  

“Could you help me pronounce your name the way you prefer?”



---



\### “I already guessed and now I need to fix it.”



It’s okay to repair the moment and move forward.



\*\*Try:\*\*  

“I realized I may have said your name incorrectly earlier. Could you help me get it right?”



---



\### “I forget names easily.”



Using the name right away and hearing it again in conversation helps it stay in memory.



\*\*Try:\*\*  

“Thanks, \_\_\_ — I appreciate you telling me.”



---



\### “The space is noisy or online audio is unclear.”



Written cues can help.



\*\*Try:\*\*  

“Would you mind typing the pronunciation in the chat so I can repeat it back?”



---



Learning names is something we all get better at with practice. Each small effort contributes to a campus where people feel recognized and welcomed.
