HERO_HEADING
Belonging starts with names.

HERO_BODY
At TRU—most days begin with names: an instructor calling roll, a student joining a group, a quick hello at a service counter, a message in an email thread, or a Teams call where the audio cuts out at the worst moment. In those small moments, a name is often the first thing we try to say out loud about someone.

When a person hears their name said the way they prefer, the interaction softens. It signals attention, respect, and care. Over time, those everyday signals help shape campus as a place of belonging.

Learning names can feel uncomfortable at first. Many of us worry we’ll get it wrong, slow things down, or make things awkward. That’s normal. Names are a social skill, not a test—and like any social skill, they get easier with practice.

This tab is here to help make that practice simpler: a few stories, a few short how‑to reminders, and a tool you can use as a starting point.

### Quick guide
Most name moments don’t need a script or a performance. A warm check, a brief repeat, and a willingness to adjust is usually enough. Treat names the way you treat any new word you want to learn: normal, respectful, and worth the effort.

• Ask early, with permission  
• Listen once, then repeat once  
• Try again briefly if needed  
• Use the name naturally in your next sentence  
• Adjust and continue (thank them, carry on)

LEARNING_HEADING
Learning names

LEARNING_BODY
Learning names is a shared campus skill. The goal is not flawless pronunciation on the first try—the goal is ease: fewer awkward guesses, fewer repeat corrections, and more comfort in introductions.

### A practical sequence (the “4‑step loop”)
1. **Ask with permission.** Ask early, before a guess becomes a habit.  
2. **Listen once, then reflect.** Let the person say it clearly, then repeat it back at a normal pace.  
3. **Invite one more try.** A second attempt often helps it stick.  
4. **Use it right away.** Use the name in your next sentence, then again later.

### In‑the‑moment scripts (pick the lightest one that fits)
• “Could you help me pronounce your name the way you prefer?”  
• “I want to get this right. Can I try it once more?”  
• “Where does the emphasis go?”  
• “Would you like to tell me, or should I try first?”

### Facilitation guidance (instructors, hosts, front‑facing staff)
**Before introductions:** set a brief norm once. “Quick corrections are welcome. Thank you for helping me learn.”  
**During introductions:** give options and reduce spotlighting (voice, chat, or a private note).  
**When you miss a name:** repair quickly and return to the topic (correct → thank → continue).

SHARING_HEADING
Sharing your name

SHARING_BODY
You deserve to be addressed the way you prefer. You also get to choose how much you want to teach, repeat, or explain. A preference is enough.

### If you want to help someone learn your name
1. Say it once normally, then once a bit slower.  
2. Break it into parts (two or three pieces is usually enough).  
3. Add emphasis (“stress on the ___”) if helpful.  
4. Invite a second try.

### Scripts that protect your energy
• “I go by ___.”  
• “It’s pronounced ___.”  
• “Almost. It’s ___.”  
• “Please call me ___.” (That’s a complete sentence.)

### Supporting others to share (facilitation)
Offer multiple ways to share (spoken, chat, private note). Don’t ask for origins or “where it’s from.” Normalize updates: names and preferences can change over time.

BARRIERS_HEADING
Barriers and bridges

BARRIERS_BODY
Below are common barriers people feel, and small bridges that help. Use the script if it fits your voice.

1. **Barrier:** “I’m nervous to ask. It might feel awkward.” **Bridge:** Asking early is usually less awkward than guessing later. **Script:** “Can I quickly check how you say your name?”
2. **Barrier:** “I’m worried I’ll say it wrong and offend someone.” **Bridge:** Make it collaborative and low‑stakes. **Script:** “Help me get this right. Can I try it once more?”
3. **Barrier:** “I guessed, and now I need to fix it.” **Bridge:** Repair calmly and move on. **Script:** “Thanks for your patience. Could you say it again?”
4. **Barrier:** “I don’t want to make a big deal out of someone’s name.” **Bridge:** Treat it like learning any new word: brief, normal, respectful. **Script:** “I want to say it the way you prefer. How do you pronounce it?”
5. **Barrier:** “I forget names, especially in big groups.” **Bridge:** Use the name right away; a second use helps it stick. **Script:** “Thanks, ___.” (Use it again naturally in your next sentence.)
6. **Barrier:** “Corrections derail the flow when I’m teaching or facilitating.” **Bridge:** Set a norm once so corrections stay short. **Script:** “Quick corrections are welcome. Thank you.”
7. **Barrier:** “It’s a busy service environment. There’s no time.” **Bridge:** Use a fast preference check. **Script:** “What name should I use today?”
8. **Barrier:** “Reading unfamiliar names is hard for me.” **Bridge:** Lead with listening, not guessing from spelling. **Script:** “Could you say your name for me? I want to get it right.”
9. **Barrier:** “I have a speech difference; some sounds are hard.” **Bridge:** Ask for coaching without turning it into a performance. **Script:** “I may need a moment to practice. Could you break it into parts?”
10. **Barrier:** “The space is loud / I’m hard of hearing.” **Bridge:** Reduce noise or confirm in writing. **Script:** “Could you type the syllables so I can repeat it back?”
11. **Barrier:** “Online audio is glitchy and I can’t catch it.” **Bridge:** Use chat as backup and confirm once audio is clear. **Script:** “Could you type how you say it? I’ll repeat it back.”
12. **Barrier:** “Someone keeps using a nickname I didn’t choose.” **Bridge:** State your preference plainly; no justification required. **Script:** “Please call me ___.”
